01 — How the Engine Works
Seven-Layer Evaluation Pipeline
📐
Composite Score 0–100
Each layer produces a sub-score. Weighted layer scores combine into a final composite. The signal — STRONG BUY through STRONG SELL — maps from where that composite lands.
🧩
Asset-Class Profiles
Layer 1 (market price analysis) is shared across all asset classes. Layers 2–6 are pluggable profiles — CEF, BDC, REIT, and MLP each have different fundamentals that matter.
🛑
Layer 5 Has Veto Power
A security can score perfectly on Layers 1–4 and still receive HOLD or AVOID if the risk layer identifies structural problems. Cheap and broken is still broken.
⚡
Momentum Confirmation Gate
A wide discount without positive price momentum is a value trap, not an opportunity. BUY signals require momentum confirmation. Wide discount + falling price = WAIT, not BUY.
🔄
Self-Calibrating Over Time
Every signal is timestamped and tracked. Forward returns at 30/60/90/180 days feed back into accuracy analysis. Parameters can be recalibrated quarterly based on what the engine gets right and wrong.
🔒
Six-Month Paper Gate
No signal reaches live deployment or paid IronSignal subscribers until it has been paper-traded for six months with tracked outcomes. David approves all parameter changes before they go live.

Layer 1 is identical across all asset classes — the discount/NAV math and z-score logic don't change. What you're defining here is how strict the entry threshold is, which lookback windows matter most, and how to group CEF peers for relative comparison.
1.1What minimum discount to NAV must a CEF show before the engine considers it BUY-eligible?
This is the entry floor. A discount of −8% means the engine won't generate a BUY signal unless the fund trades at least 8% below its NAV, regardless of other factors. Tighter floor = more signals. Wider = fewer, higher-conviction.
1.2Which Z-score lookback period carries the most weight in your valuation thinking?
1-year Z tells you cheapness vs. recent conditions. 3-year captures a full cycle. 5-year gives maximum context but can anchor to historically abnormal environments (e.g., post-COVID distortions).
1.3Momentum gate — how strict should this be for CEFs?
The current live engine blocks ~87.5% of buy candidates with a strict momentum requirement. Your investment philosophy is value-focused — wide discounts are opportunities. The question is how much you want momentum to gate discount-driven signals.
1.4Peer group structure for relative discount comparison:
Comparing a muni CEF's discount to an equity CEF's gives a meaningless signal. Narrower peer groups = more precise comparison, but smaller sample sizes.
1.5Seasonal scoring — should the engine adjust CEF scores for known seasonal patterns?
Year-end tax loss selling reliably widens CEF discounts in November/December. January effect often narrows them. The existing JS engine uses a 10% seasonal weight.
For CEFs, the fundamental questions are: Is the distribution sustainable? How much is return of capital? Does the fund have UNII cushion? What is the effective leverage under the Investment Company Act? What is the expense ratio vs. peers?
2.1UNII (Undistributed Net Investment Income) — how does it factor into your assessment of distribution sustainability?
Positive UNII = the fund has earned more than it has distributed — a cushion. Negative UNII = it's paying out more than it earns, a distribution cut risk.
2.2Return of Capital (ROC) — at what percentage of distribution does ROC become a concern?
Some ROC is benign (return of gains). Destructive ROC erodes NAV over time. The question is where your threshold is.
